Week 12: Urban Forestry & Arboriculture

What is Urban Forestry?

This section introduces the foundational concepts of managing trees in our cities. Urban forestry is the comprehensive care and management of all the trees within a municipality—what we call the Urban Tree Canopy (UTC). It's a broad field that combines science, art, and policy to ensure our city trees are healthy, safe, and beneficial.

Urban Forestry

This is the big picture. It involves planning, planting, protecting, and maintaining the entire tree population of a city. Urban foresters work with city planners, engineers, and the public to integrate trees into the urban environment. This large-scale management is key to developing Green Infrastructure—natural systems that provide essential services to our communities.

Arboriculture

This is the hands-on practice. Arboriculture focuses on the health and care of individual trees. An arborist is a tree specialist who understands the biology of trees and is trained in all aspects of tree care, including planting, pruning, diagnosing problems, and safe removal. Think of an arborist as a "tree doctor."

Together, urban forestry (the forest) and arboriculture (the trees) work to build a resilient and beneficial urban canopy that improves our quality of life.

The Role of Trees in Urban Settings

Urban trees are far more than just decoration; they are critical components of our Green Infrastructure. They provide tangible economic, environmental, and social benefits. Combating the Urban Heat Island Effect

Cities are hotter than surrounding rural areas due to pavement and buildings—this is the Urban Heat Island Effect. Trees provide shade, which cools surfaces, and release water vapor through transpiration, which cools the air. A healthy tree canopy can reduce local air temperatures by several degrees, lowering energy costs for cooling and making the city more comfortable.

Factors in Selecting Urban Trees

Choosing the right tree for the right place is the most important decision in urban forestry. A poorly chosen tree can become a liability, while a well-chosen tree will provide benefits for decades. This section covers key selection factors, including an interactive tool to filter common trees by their climate suitability.

Hardiness & Zone Maps

A tree's hardiness is its ability to survive in a specific climate, especially its tolerance to minimum winter temperatures. The USDA Zone Map divides the country into zones based on these average minimums. You must select trees rated for your hardiness zone or a colder one.

Growth Habit & Site

The growth habit refers to a tree's mature size, shape, and form (e.g., columnar, spreading, pyramidal). You must consider the available space, both above ground (for branches) and below ground (for roots). Never plant a large-growing tree under power lines or in a tiny planting strip.

Functions of Soil & Water Management

Urban soils are often terrible for tree growth—they are compacted, poorly drained, and low in organic matter. Understanding soil function is vital for tree survival. This section explores soil functions and a key tool for managing soil moisture.

Soil Functions

Healthy soil is a living ecosystem. For trees, it must perform four key functions:

  • Anchorage: Physically hold the tree upright.
  • Water: Store and provide water to the roots.
  • Nutrients: Supply essential minerals for growth.
  • Oxygen: Allow roots to respire (breathe). Compaction destroys air spaces, suffocating roots.

The Tensiometer

A tensiometer is a tool used to measure soil moisture tension. It tells an arborist *how hard* a tree's roots have to pull to get water. It is *not* a moisture meter; it measures water potential. This helps prevent both over-watering (which drowns roots) and under-watering.

Soil is moist. Water is held loosely. Gauge reads low tension.

Pruning and Structural Support

Proper care ensures trees are safe, healthy, and attractive. Pruning is the most common tree care practice, while cabling and bracing provide structural support to weak or damaged trees. Click on the cards below to learn about common pruning objectives.

How to Prune Trees

Pruning is done for specific objectives. It's not just "cutting branches." All cuts should be made just outside the branch collar to allow the tree to seal the wound properly. Never leave stubs and never make a "flush cut."

1. Cleaning

Click to learn more

Removing dead, dying, diseased, or broken branches. This is the most basic and important pruning operation for tree health and safety.

DO: Cut back to healthy wood.
DON'T: Leave diseased stubs.

2. Raising

Click to learn more

Removing lower branches to provide clearance for pedestrians, vehicles, or buildings. This is very common in urban environments.

DO: Prune gradually over time.
DON'T: Remove more than 25% of the live canopy at once.

3. Reduction

Click to learn more

Reducing the height or spread of a tree by pruning back branches to smaller, lateral branches. This is used to clear utility lines or reduce risk of failure.

DO: Cut back to a lateral branch that is at least 1/3 the diameter of the cut stem.
DON'T: "Top" the tree (indiscriminate flat cuts), which is very harmful.

Cabling and Bracing

When pruning isn't enough, arborists use hardware to stabilize trees. This is specialized work to reduce the risk of failure in trees with weak branch unions or splits.

Cables are strong steel wires installed in the upper canopy to limit the movement of branches. They are flexible and add support against wind or ice loads.

Braces are rigid steel rods installed through weak unions or splits to "bolt" the tree together. They are often used in combination with cables.

Plant Health Care (PHC) System

Plant Health Care (PHC) represents a major shift in thinking about tree management. Instead of waiting for a tree to get sick or infested (a reactive approach), PHC is a proactive, holistic system focused on keeping trees healthy from the start. This section compares PHC to older, traditional methods.

Feature Plant Health Care (PHC) Traditional Tree Care
Philosophy Proactive & Holistic: Health-focused Reactive: Pest & problem-focused
Goal Maintain optimal tree health and vitality. "Cure" a specific problem (e.g., kill insects).
Monitoring Regular site visits to monitor tree and soil health. Called out only when a problem is visible.
Treatments Focus on cultural practices (e.g., proper watering, mulching, soil improvement). Uses pesticides as a last resort. Often relies on routine chemical sprays as a primary solution.
Client Role A partner in the tree's health management plan. A customer buying a service (e.g., a spray).

PHC is a more sustainable and effective long-term approach that prioritizes prevention over reaction, treating the tree as a whole system within its environment.

Career Opportunities in Urban Forestry

Urban forestry is a rapidly growing field with diverse career paths for those who love science, nature, and working outdoors. As cities increasingly recognize the value of their urban tree canopy, the demand for skilled professionals is high. This section outlines some of the primary career opportunities available.

Municipal Arborist / Urban Forester

Works for a city or town government. Manages the *entire* urban forest. Responsibilities include planning, public tree inventory, risk assessment, managing contracts, writing tree ordinances, and community outreach.

Commercial Arborist

Works for a private tree care company. This is the hands-on "tree doctor." Roles range from ground crew to climber to plant health care technician. They prune, remove, plant, and treat trees for residential and commercial clients.

Consulting Arborist

An expert who provides specialized advice. They don't typically do the physical work but are hired to write reports, appraise tree value, diagnose complex problems, manage tree preservation on construction sites, and serve as expert witnesses.

Utility Arborist

A specialist focused on managing vegetation near power lines. This is a critical role for public safety and preventing power outages. It involves specialized pruning techniques (e.g., reductions) and vegetation management planning.

Researcher / Scientist

Works at a university, government agency, or botanical garden. Conducts research on tree biology, invasive pests, new tree cultivars, urban soil science, and the benefits of trees. This science underpins the entire industry.

Non-Profit / Community Forester

Works for a non-profit organization focused on community greening. Organizes tree planting events, educates the public, writes grants, and advocates for green space and environmental equity in underserved neighborhoods.
