Chapter 4: Forest Mensuration & Inventory

The Science of Forest Measurement

Introduction: Why We Measure Forests

In the previous chapters, we explored how forests function (Ecology) and how we can manage them to meet specific goals (Silviculture). However, none of that is possible without answering a fundamental question: "What's actually out there?" This is the realm of Forest Mensurationâ€”the science of measuring trees and forests.

Think of a forester like a doctor for the forest. Before a doctor can prescribe a treatment, they need to take measurements: your temperature, blood pressure, weight. Similarly, before a forester can write a management plan, they must conduct a Forest Inventory to quantify the forest's resources. Mensuration provides the tools and techniques to perform that inventory.

Part 1: Measuring the Individual Tree

The foundation of any forest inventory is the accurate measurement of individual trees. We focus on three primary attributes: diameter, height, and age.

Tree Diameter

Diameter is the single most important tree measurement because it's strongly correlated with a tree's volume, biomass, and value. By convention, diameter is measured at a standardized point to ensure consistency.

Key Concept: Diameter at Breast Height (DBH)

The standard for diameter measurement is Diameter at Breast Height (DBH), which is taken at 4.5 feet (1.3 meters) above the ground on the uphill side of the tree. This specific height avoids the irregular swelling common at the base of a tree.

Common Tool: Diameter Tape (D-Tape)

A D-Tape is a flexible tape measure, but instead of showing standard units, its graduations are based on the formula for circumference ($C = \pi \times D$). Every time the tape wraps $\pi$ inches (approx 3.14"), the tape reads "1 inch". This allows a forester to measure circumference and read diameter directly.

Tree Height

Tree height is another critical variable for determining volume. Two height measurements are common: Total Height (from the ground to the very top of the tree) and Merchantable Height (from the stump to a point on the stem where the tree becomes too small or defective to be used for a product).

Common Tool: Clinometer

A clinometer uses basic trigonometry to calculate height. A forester stands a known distance from the tree (e.g., 100 feet), measures the angle to the top of the tree, and measures the angle to the bottom. The clinometer automatically converts these angles into height readings.

Tree Age

Age is essential for understanding a forest's growth rate and history. While counting rings on a stump is accurate, it's not practical for living trees. Instead, foresters use a specialized tool.

Common Tool: Increment Borer

An increment borer is a hollow drill bit that extracts a small, pencil-thin core from the tree's trunk without killing it. The forester can then count the annual growth rings on this core to determine the tree's age at the height of the boring.

Part 2: Measuring the Forest

Measuring every single tree in a large forest (a census) is usually impossible. Instead, foresters rely on statistics to measure a representative sample of the forest and then extrapolate those findings to the entire area.

Sampling with Plots

An inventory is conducted by establishing a series of sample points, or plots, throughout the forest. Within each plot, every tree is measured. The data from all plots is then averaged and scaled up.

Fixed-Radius Plots

The most straightforward method. The forester establishes a circular plot of a known size (e.g., 1/10th of an acre). Every tree whose center falls within that circle is measured. This is easy to understand but can be inefficient, as you might measure many small, low-value trees.

Variable-Radius Plots (Prism Cruising)

A more advanced and highly efficient method. Using an angle gauge or glass prism, the forester stands at a single point and sights each nearby tree. The prism optically shifts the tree trunk. If the shifted image overlaps with the real trunk, the tree is "in" and tallied. This method automatically samples more large trees than small trees, biasing the sample towards the trees with the most volume and value.

Conclusion: From Measurement to Management

Forest inventory is the bridge between understanding a forest and managing it wisely. The data gatheredâ€”such as volume per acre, species composition, and average growth ratesâ€”is what informs every silvicultural decision. It allows a forester to:

  • Determine a sustainable harvest level.
  • Estimate the economic value of a timber sale.
  • Model future forest growth and yield.
  • Assess wildlife habitat suitability.
  • Plan thinning treatments to improve forest health.

Without accurate measurement, forestry is just guesswork. With it, forestry becomes a powerful science for stewarding our natural resources.
