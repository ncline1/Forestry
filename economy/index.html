Section 1: Forest Economics

Forest economics applies economic theories to understand how we manage and utilize forest resources. This section explores the foundational concepts, from the unique financial nature of trees as long-term assets to the critical decision of when to harvest for optimal financial return. We'll examine how factors like time, risk, and interest rates influence forest management.

The Forester's Dilemma: When to Harvest?

This interactive chart illustrates the core financial decision in forestry. A tree is both a factory (growing wood) and a product. The optimal economic time to harvest is when the financial growth rate slows to match what you could earn by investing the money elsewhere (the discount rate). Valuing the Priceless

Forests provide far more than just timber. They offer critical ecosystem services like clean water, air purification, and recreation, which don't have a market price. This section introduces the challenge of non-market valuation. Below, you can explore the different economic methods used to assign monetary value to these "priceless" benefits, a crucial step for ensuring they are considered in policy and land-use decisions.

Contingent Valuation

Directly asking people what they are willing to pay for an environmental good.

Travel Cost Method

Valuing a site based on how much visitors spend to get there.

Hedonic Pricing

Isolating the value of an amenity through its effect on market prices (e.g., housing).

Section 2: Forest Policy

Forest policy creates the rules and incentives that guide forest management. It's how societies translate their values—for conservation, economic use, and recreation—into action. This section covers the "why" and "how" of forest policy, from the goals it aims to achieve to the major laws that shape forestry in the United States.

Key U.S. Forest Legislation

Explore some of the landmark laws that govern forest management on public and private lands.

National Forest Management Act (NFMA)

Requires systematic, interdisciplinary planning for national forests to ensure sustainable resource management and public participation.

Endangered Species Act (ESA)

Mandates the protection of threatened and endangered species and their habitats, significantly influencing timber harvesting practices.

Clean Water Act (CWA)

Regulates water pollution, leading to the development of Best Management Practices (BMPs) to control erosion from forestry operations.
