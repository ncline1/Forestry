12.1: Watershed Management

This section explores how forestry practices are deeply connected to the health of our water resources. A watershed is an area of land that drains all its water to a common outlet, like a river or lake. Forest management can significantly affect both the quantity (yield) and quality of water flowing from these areas. We'll look at potential impacts and the "Best Management Practices" (BMPs) foresters use to protect water.

Upland Forest: Healthy, intact forests act like a sponge. Tree roots and forest floor litter help water infiltrate the soil slowly, which recharges groundwater, filters water naturally, and prevents rapid, flashy runoff that can cause erosion.
Harvest Area: Removing tree cover can increase water yield because less water is taken up by trees (transpiration). However, exposed soil is vulnerable to erosion. Building forest roads improperly is a major source of sediment, which can pollute streams.
Riparian Zone: This is the critical buffer area directly adjacent to a stream. Leaving trees and vegetation intact here is a key BMP. It shades the stream (keeping water cool for fish), filters sediment from runoff, and stabilizes the stream banks.

Key Concepts: Best Management Practices (BMPs)

BMPs are the rules and techniques foresters follow to minimize environmental impact, especially on water. Click to expand.

Click to see common forestry BMPs for water
  • Streamside Management Zones (SMZs): Maintaining a protective buffer of trees and vegetation around all streams and water bodies (the Riparian Zone).
  • Proper Road Construction: Building roads with proper drainage (like water bars and culverts) to divert water off the road surface and prevent it from carrying sediment into streams.
  • Minimize Soil Compaction: Avoiding use of heavy machinery on wet soils to prevent "compacting" the soil, which reduces water infiltration and increases runoff.
  • Revegetate Disturbed Areas: Quickly replanting or seeding areas of exposed soil (like log landings) after a harvest to stabilize the soil and prevent erosion.

12.2: Wildlife Habitat Management

Forests are more than just trees; they are complex ecosystems that provide habitat for countless wildlife species. Silviculture (the art and science of growing trees) can be a powerful tool to actively manage and enhance these habitats. This section explores how different forestry practices can be tailored to meet the needs (food, cover, water, and space) of specific wildlife.

Forest Habitat Layers

Canopy: The uppermost layer of mature trees. Home to many birds (e.g., warblers, hawks) and animals that feed on leaves, buds, or fruits (e.g., squirrels).
Understory: Smaller trees and saplings growing beneath the main canopy. Provides cover and nesting sites for birds like the Wood Thrush.
Shrub Layer: Low-growing woody plants. This layer is critical for cover and food (berries) for species like deer, grouse, and rabbits.
Forest Floor: Composed of leaf litter, herbs, fungi, and decaying wood (Coarse Woody Debris). Essential for salamanders, insects, shrews, and ground-foraging birds.

Silvicultural Tools for Habitat

Foresters can use specific techniques to create habitat features. Click the buttons to see what they are.

Click a button above to learn about a technique.

Creating Snags (Dead Standing Trees): Snags are apartment complexes for wildlife. Woodpeckers excavate cavities, which are then used by bluebirds, owls, and flying squirrels. They are also hunting perches for hawks and eagles. Foresters may "girdle" (cut the bark around) a few trees to create new snags.
Leaving Coarse Woody Debris (CWD): These are logs and large branches left on the forest floor. They provide critical habitat for insects, salamanders, and fungi. As they decay, they return nutrients to the soil. Grouse often use logs for "drumming" displays.
Managing for Mast: "Mast" refers to the fruits and nuts of trees (e.g., acorns, cherries, hickory nuts). Foresters can favor mast-producing trees like oaks during a harvest to ensure a reliable, high-energy food source for species like deer, bear, and turkey.

12.3: Forest Recreation Management

Forests provide invaluable opportunities for human recreation, from hiking and camping to hunting and birdwatching. Managing for recreation involves balancing human desires with the need to protect the forest ecosystem. This section covers the principles of matching people with the right experiences and managing the impacts they can have.

Recreation Opportunity Spectrum (ROS)

ROS is a framework managers use to provide a diverse range of recreation experiences, from "Primitive" (high solitude, no facilities) to "Urban" (high traffic, many facilities). This table outlines the concept.

Switchback: A sharp, zig-zag turn used to build a trail up a steep slope at a gentle, sustainable grade, preventing fast water flow and erosion.
Water Bar: A log, rock, or mound of soil built diagonally across a trail to intercept water and divert it off the trail surface before it can gain speed and erode the path.
